The ground computer is the system's real-time interpretation and visualization layer, reading the annotated CSV stream over serial, reconstructing the rocket's physical state, and rendering a live three-dimensional attitude model alongside a numeric telemetry overlay. Beyond simple parsing, it performs meaningful signal processing on the incoming data: barometric pressure is converted to estimated altitude using the standard atmospheric formula, and vehicle attitude is estimated using a complementary filter that fuses high-rate gyroscope integration with periodic accelerometer-derived tilt correction, weighted dynamically based on the magnitude of measured acceleration so that the filter trusts the accelerometer only when the vehicle is close to a resting one-g state and defers entirely to gyroscope integration during motor burn or free-fall, when linear acceleration would otherwise corrupt a naive tilt estimate. Time integration for this filter is anchored to the timestamp embedded in each telemetry packet by the rocket itself rather than to the ground computer's own wall-clock render loop, which decouples the accuracy of the attitude estimate from any rendering-side timing jitter introduced by the visualization or operating system. The ground computer additionally maintains its own independent assessment of link health by tracking packet sequence numbers to detect and quantify dropped packets, and by surfacing the RSSI and SNR values supplied by the ground receiver, giving an operator two complementary and independently-derived indicators of transmission quality displayed side by side in real time.
