Taken together, the three components form a single telemetry pipeline distributed across three physically separate pieces of hardware, each with a clearly bounded responsibility and no overlap of concerns: the rocket-side firmware is solely responsible for sensor acquisition, timestamping, and dual-path delivery of raw telemetry; the ground receiver is solely responsible for the physical radio link and for injecting the one piece of information — link quality — that only it is positioned to measure; and the ground computer is solely responsible for interpreting, fusing, and visualizing that data for a human operator. Data integrity is enforced at the point closest to where corruption could occur, via hardware CRC checking on the radio link itself, while a second, independent method of assessing link health, sequence-number-based loss detection, is computed downstream at the ground computer, giving the system two complementary lines of defense rather than a single point of failure in judging whether the data being displayed can be trusted. The decision to timestamp every sample at its origin on the rocket, rather than at any downstream point of reception, is what allows the ground station's attitude estimation to remain accurate regardless of variable radio latency, serial buffering delay, or rendering-loop jitter — a detail that is easy to overlook but is foundational to the correctness of every derived value shown to the operator. The overall design reflects sound engineering judgment for a live telemetry system: it separates real-time transmission from durable on-board storage so no single point of failure results in total data loss, it isolates timing-critical sensor work from lower-priority I/O work on the rocket itself, and it distributes link-quality awareness to the one node physically capable of measuring it, rather than attempting to infer it indirectly elsewhere in the pipeline.
